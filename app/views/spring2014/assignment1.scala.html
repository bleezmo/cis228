Ketchup's Modern Revolution



Ketchup's modern revolution began in 1991 in a small precision-molding shop wedged between a scrapyard and a saloon in Midland, Mich. The key prop in the dingy scene was a molding press that was meant to turn injections of liquid silicone into flexible, one-piece precision valves.

Paul Brown, the shop's stocky, bullheaded owner, spent his days sitting before the press on a four-legged stool, chain-smoking and rethinking the valves' design.

His vision was a dispensing valve for a new kind of shampoo bottle that could be stored upside down on, say, a tub's edge. The valve had to open easily when squeezed and shut securely when the squeezing stopped. No drips, no leaks â€” ever.

Brown and his mold-maker, Tim Socier, came up with a valve that's a little silicone dome with right-angled slits cut in its top. Ketchup extraction was a two step process:

  1. When the bottle's sides were pressed, the dome's slits opened like flower petals and released the contents.
  2. When the pressing stopped, the air sucked back into the dome caused it to retract, and the slits to close.

Brown sat back in his chair and said: "Holy cow, I just hit the jackpot."
